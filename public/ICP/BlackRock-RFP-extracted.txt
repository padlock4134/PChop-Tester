How to Read This RFP Guide 
This RFP Guide is organized into three parts: the Overview, the Application Questions, and the Appendix. 
The Overview provides the core narrative for the funding opportunity, including the purpose of BlackRock Future 
Builders, the types of solutions eligible for funding, applicant eligibility, grant structure, selection criteria, timeline, 
and submission process. 
The Application Questions section outlines the information applicants will be asked to submit through 
Submittable, including organization details, proposed program type, program narrative responses, budget 
information, funding sources, and required attachments. 
The Appendix provides supporting definitions, reporting guidance, and KPI reference materials to help applicants 
understand key terms, expected outcomes, and potential reporting requirements. The appendix is intended as a 
reference tool and does not replace the application questions or formal submission requirements. 
Overview 
BlackRock Future Builders is a $100M philanthropic initiative funded by The BlackRock Foundation to expand 
economic opportunity and power the next generation of America’s skilled trades workers. Through this Request for 
Proposals (RFP) process, The Foundation seeks to support U.S. nonprofit organizations (i.e., registered 501(c)3 
organizations) delivering or supporting high-quality skilled trades training. 
This competitive grant opportunity operates in parallel to other BlackRock Future Builders partnerships and is 
intended to surface additional, regionally grounded solutions across the skilled trades ecosystem. Organizations 
may propose direct worker training programs or capacity-building efforts that enable systems to function more 
effectively and at greater scale.

The BlackRock Foundation intends to commit $25 million over two funding cycles to support two-year grants 
ranging from $500,000 to $1 million through this RFP process. This first round RFP will be administered by Jobs 
for the Future (JFF), a national nonprofit that transforms U.S. education and workforce systems. 
Focus Areas & Solutions 
Applicants may propose worker training or capacity-building solutions aligned to skilled trades workforce 
development. 
Worker training interventions may include direct service proposals such as pre-apprenticeship or career 
navigation programs, workforce training programs, apprenticeship programs, or wrap-around participant supports 
that are supportive of training persistence and completion. 
Capacity-building interventions are organizational and system-focused, rather than in direct service of 
participants, and may include, but are not limited to, proposals such as ecosystem-building, demonstration 
projects, technology or tool adoptions, or research, among others. 
Eligibility 
This application is open to U.S.-based nonprofit organizations (including Puerto Rico and U.S. Virgin Islands) that 
align with The BlackRock Foundation’s charitable purpose to strengthen financial security for more people, and 
meet The Foundation’s standard eligibility requirements. 
Occupations: All skilled trades are eligible, provided applicants demonstrate a strong connection to infrastructure 
projects such as data center construction or related supply chains, energy infrastructure, or transportation 
infrastructure. Some examples include, but are not limited to, electricians, HVAC technicians, plumbers, pipefitters 
and steamfitters, ironworkers, insulation workers, and construction equipment operators.   
Organizations must: 
• Be recognized as a 501(c)(3) public charity or equivalent 
• Propose funding for a specific program or project, not general operating support 
• Demonstrate sound governance, leadership, and financial health 
Additional requirements: 
• Typically, no more than 25% of an organization’s annual budget should be directed to administrative and 
fundraising overhead 
• Applicants may request grant funding ranging from $500,000 to $1 million 
 
Grant funds may not be used for lobbying, political activity, impermissible private benefit or inurement, or any 
other non-charitable purpose. Grant recipients must use funds only for the approved program, comply with 
applicable reporting and monitoring requirements, and adhere to any additional restrictions or safeguards 
(including, where applicable, expenditure responsibility requirements) specified by The BlackRock Foundation. The 
following types of organizations are not eligible for consideration:

Grant Structure 
Grants will be awarded for a two-year period. Proposed budgets should be clearly aligned to activities, deliverables, 
and expected outcomes, and reflect efficient, cost-effective use of funds. The scope of funded work should be 
feasible within the grant timeframe and appropriately tailored to the applicant’s organizational capacity. 
Reporting & Key Performance Indicators 
Grantees will be required to submit biannual progress reports over the grant period. Reporting will focus on 
progress against a defined set of key performance indicators (KPIs) aligned to the proposed intervention, such as 
participant outcomes, system-level improvements, employer engagement, and other relevant measures of 
progress and impact. 
Selection Criteria 
Applications will be initially evaluated using a structured rubric. Core evaluation criteria include: 
• Strategic fit with regional labor demand – alignment to documented skilled trades workforce needs and 
regional priorities 
• Strength of the proposed solution – clarity of the intervention, outcomes, and connection between 
activities and impact 
• Ecosystem engagement – depth of partnerships across employers, training providers, workforce systems, 
and public agencies 
• Program design – quality of training or capacity-building models and related supports that drive access, 
persistence, and completion

Program Narrative Questions 
The BlackRock Foundation seeks proposals that directly or indirectly increase the number of individuals prepared 
for and successfully entering skilled trades careers. Applicants may include training providers, workforce 
intermediaries, sector partnerships, or other organizations or coalitions that strengthen the skilled trades talent 
ecosystem. Proposals must address how the work will lead to measurable improvements in workforce readiness, 
training capacity, job placement, retention, or wage progression. 
Proposals must fit into one of two possible program types: Worker training or capacity-building.  
“Worker training” interventions may include proposals such as pre-apprenticeship or career navigation programs, 
workforce training programs, apprenticeship programs, or wrap-around participant supports that are additive to 
training. 
“Capacity-building” interventions may include, but are not limited to, proposals such as ecosystem-building, 
demonstration projects, technology or data infrastructure, or tool adoption, among others. 
Proposals may include both worker training and capacity-building components, as long as they are both clearly 
connected to meeting the proposal’s goals.

Appendix A: RFP Narrative Key Terms & Definitions  
Completion of an Apprenticeship Program or Training Program: Completion is defined as a participant 
successfully finishing the program that results in credential attainment. For Registered Apprenticeship programs, 
completion is defined as earning a journey worker credential/journey-level certification (or equivalent program 
completion credential recognized by the sponsor/industry). For pre-apprenticeship and other training, completion 
is defined as earning the industry-recognized credential(s) tied to the program design.  
Credentials in Scope: Industry-recognized credentials (defined by the grant; reported by credential type). 
Job Placement: Placement into a Registered Apprenticeship or other certified skilled trades roles that don’t require 
apprenticeships (e.g., HVAC, carpentry). Placements must be training pathway aligned. Placements in unrelated 
occupations pathways (i.e., a job not related to the individual’s training) will not be considered for reporting 
purposes. 
Placement Window: Placement is counted when it occurs within 90 days of program completion (or within 90 days 
of exit, if applicable). 
Pre-Apprenticeship Program: A program designed to prepare individuals to enter and succeed in Registered 
Apprenticeships or other high-quality apprenticeship or work-based learning programs and ultimately careers.  
Registered Apprenticeship Program: An apprenticeship program approved by either the U.S. Department of 
Labor’s Office of Apprenticeship or by a state apprenticeship agency. Participants in Registered Apprenticeship 
programs receive on-the-job supervision and mentorship, are paid increasingly higher wages as they progress 
through their training and earn industry-recognized credentials upon successful completion of the program. 
Retention: Participant is currently active in the apprenticeship/training program and working toward credential 
attainment (active status at reporting cut-off). 
Skilled Trades: Occupations requiring a particular skill set, ability, or knowledge, including but not limited to 
electricians, HVAC technicians, plumbers, pipefitters and steamfitters, ironworkers, insulation workers, 
construction equipment operators, and other industrial skilled trades, and advanced manufacturing occupations 
explicitly connected to infrastructure, energy transition, and AI-related supply chains. 
Solutions: Any program, project, or initiative that aims to fill critical labor market gaps by training and placing 
participants directly into jobs or work-based learning programs such as an apprenticeship. 
Support Services: Services that address the interrelated barriers preventing learners from successfully completing 
work-based learning programs, such as financial literacy education, transportation, childcare, housing, and 
emergency assistance. 
Training Program (Workforce Training Program): A structured education or workforce program that prepares 
participants for high-demand skilled trades roles but is not a Registered Apprenticeship. These programs typically 
combine classroom and/or lab instruction with hands-on learning, are aligned to employer and labor market 
needs, and are designed to lead to industry-recognized credentials and/or direct employment in the skilled trades.  
Work-Based Learning: An approach to training in which a student or worker completes meaningful tasks in a 
workplace. Such programs are designed to prepare participants for full-time work and help them acquire the 
knowledge and skills they need to enter or advance in particular career fields.

Purpose and Reporting Expectations 
Grantees will collect and report performance data to document progress toward the goals of their proposed project. 
The following metrics represent standard reporting requirements.  Because this funding opportunity is designed to 
support a broad range of strategies — including worker training, system-building, capacity-building, and related 
implementation activities — not every metric in this appendix will apply to every proposal. 
This appendix provides a menu of potential reporting metrics that may be used across funded projects. In the 
application, applicants should identify the metrics that best align with their proposed activities, intended 
outcomes, and grant logic model. Final reporting expectations for selected grantees will be determined in 
partnership with The BlackRock Foundation and will reflect the specific scope, deliverables, and outcomes 
associated with each funded project. 
Grantees will not be expected to report on every metric listed below. Instead, funded projects will be assigned a 
tailored set of reporting requirements based on the nature of the work proposed. 
Reporting will generally include: 
• selected quantitative metrics aligned to the approved project scope; and 
• a brief quarterly narrative summarizing progress, implementation lessons, barriers, adaptations, and 
emerging outcomes. 
The BlackRock Foundation recognizes that grant partners operate within varied data environments, reporting 
systems, and jurisdictional requirements. Wherever possible, grantees should leverage existing data systems, 
administrative records, and program documentation to meet reporting expectations.
